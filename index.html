Description of a door, 2025
Blue tape, paper
30 x 35 in
In his essay, “Where are the missing masses? The sociology of a few mundane artifacts,” Bruno Latour writes, “walls are a nice invention, but if there were no holes in them there would be no way to get in or out–they would be mausoleums or tombs. The problem is that if you make holes in the walls, anything and anyone can get in and out (cows, visitors, dust, rats, noise).”
This piece was born out of curiosity. Someone marked the fifth floor stairwell wall of the 25 East Fifth Parsons building. It seemed like someone punched two holes in the wall and so I punched another hole. Initially, I took a picture of the found punches and left it alone. The next day, I punched another hole. The day after that, the Parsons maintenence faculty covered the holes with blue tape. I punched another hole below the tape and covered it with tape myself. The next day, I discovered that the tape had been replaced by a sheet of cardboard screwed into the wall. The cardboard was cut in my favorite shape. It was amazing. I printed letter-sized images of my documentation and taped it next to the cardboard-covered wall punches. I measured the size of the cardboard sheet and printed an image of the initial three punches (the two I found and my addition) and hung it onto the sheet of cardboard by its screws. I removed it that night and the next day, the wall was plastered over. When I presented this piece for critique in thesis, my professor, Catherine Telford Keogh, who is also the Director of the Parsons Bachelors of Fine Arts Program, inferred that this piece poses ethical concerns. I damaged school property and intervened with the Parsons maintenance faculty who had to repair the wall. My peers said that it felt icky to talk about the work because I essentially forced the people who work in that building to participate in my art without consent; that this was an unwilling collaboration. Shortly after a private meeting with Catherine following the critique, I got contacted by the Director of Student Conduct and Community Standards to attend a hearing for my case on March 5, 2025. The hearing went very well. I recorded and transcribed it entirely. I was told that I should expect to receive a fine from the school after two weeks maximum. On April 22, 2025, I received an email from the Director of Student Conduct and Community Standards requiring that I write a 650-700 word “Educational Paper” reflecting on the incident due May 6, 2025. I wrote it, with support from confidential sources, emailed it to the Director, and received a very pleasant reply. My classmates found this piece hilarious and one of them signed their name on it. The plastered area of the wall is still distinct. So, this piece continues.
